\begin{abstract}
Probabilistic traffic forecasting is often evaluated by aggregate metrics
such as the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS), yet such averages can
hide safety-critical failures under incident-driven regime shifts. This paper
studies this issue through the lens of mean-preserving residual probabilistic
forecasting: a deterministic traffic backbone supplies a frozen point forecast,
and a probabilistic residual layer models uncertainty around that mean. Because
closely related residual-diffusion and residual-regression paradigms have
recently appeared in CARD, RDIT, FALDA/DMRR, and other residual-calibration
work \cite{han2022card,lai2025rdit,wang2025falda,qi2026diffusionresidual}, we do
not claim mean-preserving residual diffusion as a generic methodological
novelty. Instead, we use it as a controlled experimental scaffold for traffic
systems: all residual models share the same frozen backbone, the same scaler,
and the same splits, allowing us to separate point-forecast quality from
uncertainty calibration. We do not claim a single probabilistic model that wins
under every scoring rule. Instead, the evidence supports a robustness-oriented
selection: closed-form heteroscedastic Gaussian residual calibration
(Gaussian-B) is the residual generator that remains reliable across datasets,
scoring rules, and seeds, whereas the diffusion residual's apparent advantage is
dataset-specific and fragile. On PeMS08, its ranking changes with CRPS,
coverage, and Winkler-style interval criteria; on PeMS04, it collapses under the
same mean-preserving protocol. We further show that aggregate CRPS can mask
severe conditional under-coverage on drop subsets, motivating an incident-regime
evaluation protocol with validation-selected calibration, per-horizon
reliability analysis, paired window-level statistics, and a mean-vs-dispersion
root-cause decomposition.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Reliable traffic forecasts are central to intelligent transportation systems
(ITS), where operators must reason not only about the most likely future
traffic state, but also about uncertainty under disruptions such as crashes,
weather anomalies, and demand surges. Modern graph neural networks and
Transformer-based forecasters have substantially improved deterministic
traffic prediction by modeling road-network topology and temporal dynamics.
At the same time, diffusion-based and other probabilistic models have made it
possible to report predictive distributions rather than only point forecasts.

Despite these advances, a practical gap remains between aggregate benchmark
performance and deployment-facing reliability. In many traffic studies,
probabilistic performance is summarized by aggregate metrics such as the
continuous ranked probability score (CRPS). Such aggregate scores are useful,
but they can conceal safety-critical failure modes: a model may achieve a
competitive average CRPS while assigning near-zero coverage to rare but
operationally important incident-driven drops. For traffic management, this
conditional failure is often more consequential than a small aggregate CRPS
improvement.

This paper therefore revisits probabilistic traffic forecasting from a
failure-mode and calibration perspective. We study a modular
mean-preserving residual forecasting paradigm. A deterministic backbone such
as PDFormer or AGCRN produces a frozen point forecast, and a residual
probabilistic layer models uncertainty around that forecast. During inference,
residual samples are centered so that the predictive ensemble mean matches
the deterministic backbone. This design intentionally preserves point
accuracy and isolates the probabilistic model's role: it should calibrate
uncertainty, not silently improve or degrade the mean forecast.

This formulation is related to recent decoupled deterministic--stochastic
forecasting methods such as CARD, RDIT, and FALDA/DMRR
\cite{han2022card,lai2025rdit,wang2025falda,qi2026diffusionresidual}. We therefore do not
claim mean preservation or residual diffusion alone as a new generic
time-series mechanism. Instead, our focus is ITS-specific: we instantiate and
stress-test this paradigm on graph traffic forecasting, compare diffusion
residuals against simpler heteroscedastic Gaussian residuals, and evaluate
whether aggregate calibration transfers to incident/drop regimes. This
positioning is important because a simpler Gaussian residual layer may be
preferable if diffusion does not provide a measurable advantage where it
matters.

The experiments lead to three findings. First,
mean-preserving residual models preserve the frozen backbone's point MAE/RMSE
by construction, so probabilistic comparisons can be made without silently
changing the deterministic forecast. Second, the residual-generator ranking is
not stable enough to justify a diffusion deployment claim: PeMS08 exposes
criterion sensitivity among CRPS, coverage, and Winkler score, while PeMS04
removes the ambiguity by showing systematic diffusion under-coverage and stable
Gaussian-B behavior across seeds. The deployable method therefore pivots to
Gaussian-B as the robust residual-calibration layer, not as an aggregate-CRPS
winner over all external probabilistic baselines. Third,
incident/drop subsets reveal a severe
conditional-coverage failure that is largely hidden by aggregate metrics. This
motivates validation-selected calibration, multi-seed paired statistics, and
conformal calibration as necessary components of a trustworthy traffic
uncertainty evaluation.

The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We provide a controlled, same-backbone evaluation protocol for
    mean-preserving residual probabilistic traffic forecasting, comparing
    residual diffusion, heteroscedastic Gaussian residuals, conformal residual
    calibration, and residual-regression baselines under identical frozen
    point forecasts.
    \item We introduce a validation-only generator-selection gate and report
    its criterion sensitivity rather than hiding it. The final deployable
    residual layer is selected by robustness across datasets, scoring rules, and
    evaluation settings, which favors closed-form Gaussian-B over residual
    diffusion.
    \item We introduce an incident/drop regime evaluation with explicit subset
    size, paired window-level diagnostics, and per-horizon reliability metrics, showing why
    aggregate CRPS alone is insufficient for deployment-facing ITS uncertainty.
    \item We propose a mean-vs-dispersion root-cause decomposition that
    distinguishes failures caused by unpredictable mean shifts from failures
    caused by overly narrow predictive intervals. This decomposition clarifies
    when variance calibration can help and when it cannot.
    \item We report semantic/event conditioning as an optional diagnostic
    factor rather than a headline contribution, retaining it only if it improves
    incident-regime reliability beyond non-semantic volatility or change-point
    indicators.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Deterministic Spatio-Temporal Traffic Forecasting}
Traffic forecasting has evolved from statistical models toward deep
spatio-temporal learning over urban sensor networks. Graph-based models such
as STGCN, DCRNN, GWNet, and AGCRN encode road-network dependencies
\cite{yu2018stgcn,li2018dcrnn,wu2019graphwavenet,bai2020agcrn}, while
attention and Transformer-based architectures such as GMAN, PDFormer, and
STAEformer further improve long-range temporal and spatial modeling
\cite{zheng2020gman,jiang2023pdformer,liu2023staeformer}. These
models are often strong deterministic point forecasters. In this work they
serve as frozen mean predictors. A key requirement for our protocol is that
these backbones must be reproduced at literature-level accuracy; otherwise,
the residual distribution and all downstream probabilistic comparisons are
confounded by a weak mean.

\subsection{Residual Probabilistic Forecasting and Diffusion}
Probabilistic forecasting extends point prediction to predictive
distributions. CSDI, TimeGrad, and PriSTI are representative diffusion-based
models for multivariate time series or spatiotemporal imputation
\cite{tashiro2021csdi,rasul2021timegrad,liu2023pristi}, and DiffSTG and
SpecSTG adapt diffusion or spectral generative modeling to traffic graphs
\cite{wen2023diffstg,lin2024specstg}. A closely related generic
line of work, including CARD, RDIT, FALDA/DMRR, and diffusion residual
modeling for long-term time-series forecasting
\cite{han2022card,lai2025rdit,wang2025falda,qi2026diffusionresidual}, decouples a
strong deterministic point estimator from residual or stochastic uncertainty
modeling. These works establish that adding uncertainty modeling to a point
forecaster often preserves the point estimate, and that calibrated residual
Gaussian or diffusion models can substantially improve probabilistic scores.
This prior art overlaps strongly with any generic claim of ``mean-preserving
residual diffusion.'' Therefore, our paper treats this paradigm as an
experimental scaffold rather than as a new generic method. The remaining
traffic-specific question is different: whether such residual probabilistic
models, including residual-scale calibration and conformal residual
calibration, are reliable under graph-structured incident/drop regimes.

\subsection{Calibration, Conformal Prediction, and Incident Reliability}
For deployment, a predictive interval is useful only if its empirical
coverage matches the nominal risk level while remaining sufficiently sharp.
Metrics such as CRPS, prediction interval coverage probability (PICP), mean
prediction interval width (MPIW), Winkler score, and reliability diagrams
measure different aspects of this trade-off. Conformal prediction provides a
post-hoc route to marginal coverage guarantees and is especially relevant
when probabilistic models are overconfident. However, marginal calibration
alone may still fail conditionally on rare regimes such as incident-driven
traffic drops. We therefore evaluate both aggregate calibration and
incident/drop-subset reliability.

\subsection{External Semantics for Traffic Forecasting}
Language models and semantic urban data can encode weather, incidents,
calendar events, and regional context. Earlier versions of this work treated
semantic prompting as the central mechanism. Our evidence does not
support a universal semantic-improvement claim: no-semantic variants are
competitive or stronger on aggregate metrics, and naive semantic tail modules
can degrade calibration. We therefore restrict semantics to an optional
conditioning source in incident analysis and distinguish positive findings
from negative ones.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Let $X_{t-L+1:t}\in\mathbb{R}^{L\times N\times F}$ denote the historical
traffic observations over $N$ sensors and $F$ input features. The goal is to
predict the future sequence $Y_{t+1:t+H}\in\mathbb{R}^{H\times N\times 1}$
and its predictive distribution. We evaluate both point metrics
(MAE/RMSE/MAPE) and probabilistic metrics (CRPS, NLL, PICP, MPIW, and
Winkler score) after inverse transformation to the physical traffic scale.

\subsection{Frozen Mean Predictor}
A deterministic spatio-temporal backbone $f_\phi$ produces a point forecast
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mu} = f_\phi(X_{t-L+1:t}),
\end{equation}
where $\phi$ is frozen throughout residual training. The backbone may be
AGCRN, PDFormer, or another validated deterministic forecaster. In the final
protocol, the frozen backbone must be evaluated through the same data split,
scaler, and metric implementation used by the residual model. This prevents
``impossible'' improvements in point MAE from evaluation mismatch.

\subsection{Standardized Residual Target}
The residual is defined in the normalized forecasting space:
\begin{equation}
    R = Y - \hat{\mu}.
\end{equation}
We estimate per-horizon residual statistics on the training set only,
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{R}_{h} = \frac{R_h - m_h}{s_h + \varepsilon},
\end{equation}
where $m_h$ and $s_h$ are cached and reused for validation/test inference.
This standardization keeps the residual target compatible with the diffusion
noise schedule and avoids test leakage.

\subsection{Residual Diffusion}
The diffusion model is trained on the standardized residual $\tilde{R}$. At
inference, it produces residual samples
$\{\tilde{R}^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^{S}$. After inverse residual standardization, we
center the residual samples along the ensemble dimension:
\begin{equation}
    R^{(j)} \leftarrow R^{(j)} - \frac{1}{S}\sum_{q=1}^{S} R^{(q)}.
\end{equation}
The final predictive ensemble is
\begin{equation}
    \hat{Y}^{(j)} = \hat{\mu} + R^{(j)}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, the ensemble mean equals the frozen deterministic forecast by
construction. This is a deliberate design choice: point accuracy is inherited
from the backbone, and the residual layer is judged by uncertainty quality.

\subsection{Heteroscedastic Residual Scale (Optional)}
We optionally use a residual scale factor $a_{h,n}$, parameterized by horizon,
node, or node groups, to control predictive spread:
\begin{equation}
    R^{(j)} \leftarrow a_{h,n} R^{(j)}.
\end{equation}
The scale is selected on validation data. Test-set
sweeps are reported only as diagnostic curves, not as headline results. In our
experiments the deployable configurations use a single global, validation-selected
scale rather than a node- or group-heteroscedastic head; the per-node
parameterization is retained only as an optional extension and is not required by
any reported conclusion.

\subsection{Gaussian and Global-Widening Baselines}
To test whether diffusion is necessary, we compare against a
heteroscedastic Gaussian residual model using the same frozen mean and
residual scale. We also evaluate globally widened intervals. These controls
answer two reviewer-critical questions: whether diffusion improves beyond a
simple parametric residual, and whether coverage gains are merely the result
of wider intervals.

\subsection{Incident-Conditioned Tail Calibration (Optional)}
We optionally attach a tail-scale head $g_\psi$ that amplifies residual
variance under event conditions:
\begin{equation}
    a^{\mathrm{tail}}_{h,n}=1+\mathrm{softplus}(g_\psi(c_{t,n,h})),
\end{equation}
where $c_{t,n,h}$ may be a non-semantic volatility/change-point signal or a
semantic event embedding. The main residual model is frozen when training
this head. This module is treated as a hypothesis under test, not as an
assumed contribution. In our experiments it does not improve incident/drop
coverage without unacceptable aggregate degradation, so it is reported as a
negative finding and is excluded from the deployable configuration.

\subsection{Mean-vs-Dispersion Root-Cause Decomposition}
For an event subset, interval failure can arise from two different mechanisms:
(1) the frozen mean misses an abrupt regime shift, or (2) the predictive
spread is too narrow around an otherwise reasonable mean. To separate these
mechanisms, let $[L_{h,n}, U_{h,n}]$ denote a central prediction interval,
$c_{h,n}=(L_{h,n}+U_{h,n})/2$ its center, and
$w_{h,n}=(U_{h,n}-L_{h,n})/2$ its half-width. For each target
$Y_{h,n}$, we compute
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{h,n}=\frac{|Y_{h,n}-\hat{\mu}_{h,n}|}{w_{h,n}+\varepsilon}.
\end{equation}
Large $\rho$ indicates that the target lies far outside the interval because
of a mean-level surprise rather than a minor dispersion error. We report the
median and upper quantiles of $\rho$ on normal, spike, and drop regimes. If
incident failures are dominated by very large $\rho$, then variance-only
calibration methods such as global widening, EAE/CO-style scaling, or tail
scale heads cannot recover conditional coverage without globally sacrificing
sharpness. This decomposition is used to determine whether a failure should
be addressed by mean correction, variance calibration, or reported as an
irreducible regime surprise under the available inputs.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Protocol and Integrity Checks}
All reported probabilistic metrics are computed after inverse transforming to
the physical traffic scale. Earlier test-swept or scale-inconsistent numbers
are excluded. We enforce five checks before a result is eligible for the main
paper: (i) the bare frozen backbone and the mean-preserving residual model
must have identical point MAE/RMSE when the point estimate is the ensemble
mean; (ii) frozen backbones must be reproduced at literature-level accuracy;
(iii) scale or conformal parameters are selected on validation data only;
(iv) CRPS is computed with the same number of samples across probabilistic
models; and (v) event-subset results report sample size and paired
window-level diagnostics, with bootstrap confidence intervals when estimated.
These checks are designed to avoid impossible point
improvements, test-selection leakage, and unit-mismatched CRPS values.

\subsection{Datasets and Backbones}
We evaluate on PeMS04 and PeMS08. A critical precondition is reproducing or
explicitly bounding the frozen deterministic backbone at literature-level
accuracy. AGCRN on PeMS04 reproduces the reported benchmark level. For PeMS08,
because the PDFormer backbone does not reproduce the published point accuracy
within the stated tolerance (see the Backbone Reproduction Audit), we keep
same-backbone residual comparisons separate from external-number comparisons and
treat PeMS08 external-number comparisons as diagnostic rather than
same-backbone superiority claims.

\subsection{Same-Backbone Baselines}
To avoid attributing a strong frozen mean to the residual layer, the final
comparison uses the same deterministic backbone whenever possible. We include:
(1) the bare frozen backbone; (2) heteroscedastic Gaussian residuals;
(3) residual diffusion; (4) split-conformal residual intervals; (5)
residual-scale calibration, including EAE/CO-style validation
calibration; and (6) external probabilistic baselines such as CSDI, PriSTI,
DiffSTG, and SpecSTG when artifacts are available. When a baseline cannot be conditioned on the same
backbone, we report this limitation explicitly and avoid causal claims about
method superiority.

\subsection{Gate Status: Criterion-Robust Selection}
The diffusion-versus-Gaussian decision must be aligned with the paper's stated
objective. If the contribution is calibration reliability rather than
aggregate-CRPS SOTA, the validation gate must predeclare its scoring rule and
must not be changed after inspecting test results. The evidence shows
that no single PeMS08 rule alone gives a stable diffusion win: CRPS favors
Gaussian-B, Winkler favors Gaussian-A on the completed seeds, and marginal PICP
makes diffusion look competitive but with much wider intervals. We therefore
use the gate as a criterion-robustness diagnostic, not as a test-informed knob.

The validation-only, same-window G1 artifacts confirm this objective mismatch:
CRPS selects Gaussian-B, aggregate coverage error selects diffusion, and Winkler
selects Gaussian-A. The paper therefore frames this instability as evidence
against a diffusion deployment claim and evaluates robustness across datasets
and seeds.

We additionally ran the formal PeMS08 full-test unified comparison,
reported in Table~\ref{tab:pems08_unified_fulltest}, with
$S=20$ samples on the same 3537 test window identifiers for Gaussian-B,
diffusion, CSDI, and PriSTI. This closes the PeMS08 test-side diagnostic but
does not overturn the validation-honest generator-selection rule. On the full
test split, raw diffusion has lower CRPS than Gaussian-B (11.477 versus 11.761;
paired Gaussian-B$-$diffusion $\Delta$CRPS 0.284, 95\% CI [0.270, 0.299]) but
substantially worse PICP90 (0.713 versus 0.849). CSDI is also lower in CRPS
(11.435) but under-covers more severely (PICP90 0.685). Gaussian-B is therefore
not the PeMS08 aggregate-CRPS winner; its advantage is calibration-oriented:
better coverage and the best Winkler score among the unified full-test rows
(107.062 versus 109.251 for diffusion and 110.720 for CSDI). This result
sharpens the paper's central claim: the robustness analysis selects Gaussian-B
as the reliable residual-calibration layer, while full-test CRPS alone would
favor under-covering generative baselines.

\subsection{Ablation: Why Not Residual Diffusion?}
The uncomfortable observation on PeMS08 is that the ``best'' residual generator
depends on which validation criterion is used. Across three Winkler-aware
validation seeds, closed-form Gaussian-B has the lowest CRPS, diffusion is
closest to nominal aggregate PICP90 on the recalibrated gate subset, and the scale-matched Gaussian-A arm has
the lowest Winkler interval score. Rather than hiding this sensitivity by
reporting only the criterion most favorable to our method, we treat it as a
deployment-relevant finding: a residual generator whose apparent strength moves
between CRPS, coverage, and interval score is not robustly dominant for a
deployment objective. Table~\ref{tab:pems08_criterion_seeds} records the
PeMS08 three-way verdicts used in this analysis; no test result is used to
change the criterion.

\subsection{PeMS04 Coverage-Collapse Audit}
The unified PeMS04 AGCRN residual-diffusion row in Table~\ref{tab:trusted_main}
is the raw, pre-scale-audit residual-diffusion verdict. It is severely
under-covered (PICP90 about $0.184$), with MPIW90 only about $7.784$ despite MAE
near $19.35$. We therefore treat it as an integrity audit rather than a minor
calibration detail, and we also report the validation-only scale correction
rather than hiding it. A validation-only scale sweep with the same no-semantic
checkpoint shows that the collapse is dominated by over-shrunk residual samples:
increasing the residual sample scale from 1.0 to 3.0 raises PICP90 from 0.535
to 0.880 and reduces Winkler score from 214.971 to 145.972. A finer sweep shows
that scale 3.2 is the closest target-coverage setting (PICP90 0.895), while
3.4 and 3.6 over-cover and monotonically increase CRPS and interval width.
Table~\ref{tab:pems04_scale_audit} records this diagnostic result.

This scale audit is not evidence that the raw diffusion row was chosen to make
diffusion look weak. It shows the opposite: diffusion coverage can be repaired
by a validation-selected global scale, but the repair expands MPIW90 from about
$7.8$ to $102.530$ (roughly $13\times$ the raw width) and is still wider and
less favorable than Gaussian-B in the unified residual verdict (MPIW90 $77.161$,
CRPS $15.121$, Winkler about $139.1$). Thus the corrected diffusion diagnostic
restores marginal coverage at a substantial sharpness cost; it does not provide
a sharper or more robust deployable residual layer than Gaussian-B.

To avoid cross-configuration leakage, the PeMS04 no-semantic test row uses the
scale selected only by the no-semantic validation sweep: among the audited
scales, 3.2 is closest to nominal PICP90 without crossing into clear
over-coverage. The semantic rows at the same scale are reported only as a
diagnostic semantic-vs-no-semantic probe, not as a separately selected
configuration. They reveal a CRPS--coverage trade-off rather than a PeMS04
win: semantic conditioning lowers CRPS (15.025 on test) but under-covers
(PICP90 0.842), whereas the no-semantic selected row approaches nominal
coverage (PICP90 0.887) at substantially higher CRPS and width. Therefore
PeMS04 is not used as a positive SOTA claim; it is evidence for calibration
integrity auditing and for the cost of restoring coverage.

\subsection{Incident/Drop Failure-Mode Analysis}
The strongest ITS-specific hypothesis is that aggregate CRPS hides severe
incident/drop failures. In PeMS08 drop subsets, globally widened intervals and
Gaussian residuals still exhibit near-zero coverage under large traffic drops.
We distinguish dispersion-level failures from mean-level failures. If the
failure is mainly dispersion-level, calibrated scale or conformal intervals can
recover coverage with controlled sharpness. If the failure is mean-level, then
variance-only methods fail even when intervals are widened, implying that
incident-aware mean correction or additional causal inputs are necessary. The
formal protocol is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item define drop/incident windows reproducibly, either from incident logs
    or a pre-specified flow-drop rule, and explicitly state whether the subset
    is outcome-conditioned;
    \item report subset size, MAE, CRPS, PICP, MPIW, Winkler score, and paired
    window-level diagnostics;
    \item compare residual diffusion, Gaussian residuals, conformal residuals,
    residual-scale calibration, and external baselines where artifacts exist;
    \item report the mean-vs-dispersion ratio $\rho$ to determine whether the
    miss is caused by the frozen mean or by insufficient interval width.
\end{itemize}

The PeMS08 event-subset results support the mean-shift interpretation.
Table~\ref{tab:event_refresh} reports a refreshed event evaluation using a
deduplicated set of event-local forecast positions. The drop subset is small
($36$ forecast points from $3$ events), so we treat it as a diagnostic stress
test rather than a population-level estimate. Nevertheless, the pattern is
consistent across probabilistic families. On severe drop windows, Gaussian-B,
PriSTI, and same-backbone residual diffusion have PICP90 equal to 0.000, while
CSDI reaches only 0.028. The drop CRPS values remain extremely large
(Gaussian-B 220.360, CSDI 225.201, PriSTI 192.215, diffusion 237.865), and the
Winkler scores are in the thousands. In contrast, spike windows are covered well
by external baselines and by diffusion. This asymmetry indicates that drop
events are dominated by a mean-level regime shift rather than simply
insufficient variance.

\subsection{Backbone Reproduction Audit}
PeMS08 deterministic-backbone reproduction is treated separately from
residual-generator selection. Our final full-official PDFormer reruns, using
the official model width and 200 training epochs, improve over the initial
frozen backbone but do not reproduce the published PeMS08 point accuracy within
the pre-specified $+3\%$ tolerance: the best rerun reaches MAE/RMSE
$14.764/24.937$, and the second seed reaches $14.880/24.998$, compared with the
published $13.583/23.505$ and the tolerance bounds $13.990/24.210$. We therefore
interpret PeMS08 external-number comparisons as diagnostic rather than as
same-backbone superiority claims. The main probabilistic conclusions rely on
comparisons that preserve the same frozen mean within each dataset and
explicitly separate calibration quality from deterministic point-forecast
quality.

\subsection{Negative Results and Semantic Conditioning}
Earlier semantic prompt variants did not consistently improve aggregate
forecasting, and naive incident-tail variants degraded full-test CRPS while
failing to recover drop coverage. We therefore remove semantic prompting from
the headline method. Semantic embeddings are evaluated only as one possible
event-conditioning source against non-semantic volatility/change-point
signals; in our evidence they do not provide a stable headline
improvement and are therefore reported as a negative finding.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper is repositioned as a diagnostic study of residual probabilistic
traffic forecasting under incident regimes. Because recent work such as CARD, RDIT, FALDA/DMRR, and diffusion residual
modeling already establishes much of the generic residual-diffusion and
residual-calibration paradigm, our contribution
must be judged by traffic-specific evidence: same-backbone fairness,
validation-honest calibration, incident/drop reliability, and root-cause
analysis. The evidence does not support a simple aggregate-CRPS SOTA
claim. On the formal PeMS08 unified full-test comparison, both CSDI and
diffusion have lower CRPS than Gaussian-B, but they under-cover substantially,
whereas Gaussian-B has better coverage and lower Winkler score. On PeMS04,
however, the residual-generator ambiguity disappears: Gaussian-B beats diffusion
under CRPS, coverage, and Winkler across all three seeds while preserving the
same point MAE/RMSE.

The final claim is therefore robustness-oriented. We do not claim a single
probabilistic model that wins under every scoring rule or against every external
baseline. Instead, among same-backbone residual generators, closed-form
Gaussian-B is the deployable calibration layer because it avoids the
cross-criterion, cross-setting, and cross-dataset fragility observed for
diffusion: diffusion is closest to nominal coverage on the PeMS08 recalibrated
gate subset, but under-covers on PeMS08 full test and collapses on PeMS04. We
separate same-backbone residual comparisons from external-number comparisons,
report external generative baselines as distinct model families, and treat
the deterministic-backbone reproduction gap as an explicit limitation that does
not alter the same-frozen-mean calibration comparisons.
